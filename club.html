The problem

Our drone's motor mounts were originally a 4-piece machined aluminum assembly — one per motor, four per airframe. They worked, but at 114 g each they were carrying weight the airframe didn't need, and every gram on a quad-motor drone gets paid for in flight time and payload capacity.

The approach

I led the mold design and manufacturing process for a forged carbon fiber replacement, using chopped-tow carbon fiber and resin instead of a traditional woven prepreg layup. Forged carbon lets you pack fiber into a complex 3D shape — like the motor's bolt circle and cable channel — in a way that's difficult with sheet layup.

  • Designed the mount geometry in SolidWorks around the motor's existing bolt pattern and mounting arm, keeping it interchangeable with the original aluminum part.
  • Ran structural simulation in SolidWorks to check the design against motor thrust and vibration loads before committing to a mold.
  • Built the mold myself, through several versions — each round adjusting draft angles, vent placement, and clamping pressure as I learned what was actually causing voids and inconsistent fiber distribution in the part.
  • Hand-packed chopped carbon tow with resin into the mold, then cured, demolded, and trimmed each part to final shape.
What changed between mold versions: early pulls had resin-starved spots near the thinner mounting arm and rough release around the bolt holes. Later mold revisions added draft angle to the side wall and repositioned the vent path, which fixed both.

The result

The finished part weighs 48 g, down from 114 g for the equivalent aluminum component — a 57.9% reduction per mount. Across the drone's four motor mounts, that's roughly 264 g of structural weight removed without changing the mounting interface or motor position.

What I'd do differently

Next iteration, I'd move to a matched-mold or vacuum-assisted process to get more consistent fiber volume fraction part-to-part — hand-packing chopped tow gives good results but it's sensitive to how evenly you distribute material before clamping.
